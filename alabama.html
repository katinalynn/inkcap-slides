What I'm drawing on

The talk I'm giving today draws on my forthcoming book, Unexpected Flourishing: Growth from Decay in the Mycelial University. My first book, Putting the Humanities PhD to Work, came out in 2020 from Duke University Press. Both books are grounded in the same core questions about graduate education, career pathways, and structural change.

Why unexpected flourishing?

Thriving doesn't always look the way we expect. What looks like rot is also essential to new growth.

  • Cycles of decay and regeneration
  • Interdependent, multispecies life
  • Mycoremediation
  • Beauty, even in decay
  • For me: critical hope

The rotting log

Universities in their current form are the substrate — the fallen log from which something new might grow.

  • Decades of public disinvestment and underfunding
  • Increasing reliance on contingent labor
  • Skyrocketing tuition and student debt
  • Persistence of structural inequities — sexism, racism, ableism, etc.
The decay is real. It may also serve as a condition for new growth.
This sounds cynical, but it's not despair — it's a diagnosis. And like a fallen log in the forest, the decay is also a condition for new growth. COVID made it very clear that the solo, prestige-oriented model doesn't work. But there's something I want to name more directly today.

A moment of acute precarity

We are facing additional challenges in our current moment:

  • Federal defunding of awarded research grants
  • Direct threats to academic freedom and shared governance
  • Targeting of international students and DEI-focused programs
  • Dismantling of programs and entire institutions
This is not a normal period of institutional stress.
And yet, this is exactly when critical thinking, community, and mycelial connection matter most.
I want to name this directly. Many of you are navigating real fear — about your funding, your programs, your colleagues' visa status, your own research. These are legitimate questions and I don't want to paper over them with optimism. But I do want to offer a framework for thinking about what's possible, even now.

Toward a mycelial university

What if we envisioned a different way of working?

  • Hyphae: liminal, small-scale, exploratory, but incredibly strong and resilient
  • Mycorrhizal networks: unexpected and deeply reciprocal partnerships
  • Fruiting bodies: ephemeral, surprising
  • Mycoremediation: digesting toxins, creating conditions for growth
Image source: Cartabia, M. et al. 2021. https://doi.org/10.3390/jof7121008
Mycelium is the main component of fungi — threads a single cell wide, growing densely in soil or decaying logs, usually unseen but everywhere under our feet. By "mycelial university" I mean one that fosters tiny, interconnected, often hidden areas of growth. One that makes space for liminal work — work that may or may not bear fruit, but that nourishes the ecosystem.

My anchor points

Moving from individual action to structural change.

  • Talking about career diversity is not enough
  • Structures reveal and reinforce values
  • Public engagement is good for students, the academy, and society
  • Joy and sustainability are possible
This is an equity question.
Who decides what is considered valuable? Whose voices are heard?
I've been working on graduate education reform for almost fifteen years, and the throughline looks so much clearer in retrospect than it did when I was moving through it. I was always just making the best decision I could within the context and constraints of the moment.

Redefining success

Prestige-based model

  • Replication
  • Research primacy
  • Hidden curriculum
  • Structural inequity

Purpose-based model

  • Mentorship accountability
  • Holistic admissions
  • Public & collaborative work
  • Wellbeing & care
Cultural change cannot happen without structural change.
Higher ed is oriented around prestige, and that makes it very hard to make space for other value systems. This is where the hidden curriculum comes into play — some people come in knowing more about the system than others. There are ways to foster greater equity and more creative scholarship just by breaking down some of these tacit expectations.

Navigating
Where We Are

Some of this is harm reduction. The system is broken. Grad students have an important voice in reform — but you're also in a precarious position. It's important to be a bit selfish.
I want to be practical here. Not because the structural questions don't matter — they do — but because you need tools to navigate where you are right now, not just a vision for where we hope to get.

Celebrating smallness

Rather than becoming frozen by the scale of change needed, zoom in to the countless small interactions where you can have an impact.

  • Quiet acts of change, resistance, and support have immeasurable impact
  • What we practice at small scale, we build at large scale
adrienne maree brown calls this fractal thinking in Emergent Strategy — the patterns we're working toward at the broad scale should also be present in the smallest ones.
Sometimes we need to think small. Yes, we need broad-scale change. But rather than become frozen by how daunting that seems, sometimes it's worth zooming in to think about the countless small interactions where we CAN have an impact. Through my students, I learned to value things I might otherwise brush off as small — because quiet acts of change, resistance, and support can have immeasurable impact.

What does thriving look like for you?

Forget what formal academic success is supposed to look like. Take one minute to jot down:
  • A book, film, or song you love
  • A person whose work you admire — work defined broadly
  • One thing you'd spend more time on if you could
Give them a genuine minute. Then ask: what patterns do you see? Which questions were hard, and why? Do these responses align with what you see in higher ed? The way you respond gives a glimpse of what thriving looks like for you — and taking that seriously is part of how we shift collectively from survival mode toward the possibility of flourishing.

Discernment and discovery

How do you even know what you might want to pursue? Here are some of my favorite (free!) resources:

  • Finding Your Purpose — zine/workbook by Hannah Alpert Abrams
  • Imagine PhD — robust, research-based career exploration tool
  • YearCompass — guided reflection and goal-setting
  • Institutional resources, like the U of A Career Center or Crimson Odyssey mentoring program
  • For more: bit.ly/inkcap-resources
Tap into the curiosity that brought you to graduate school in the first place.
These multiple, creative career pathways sound great, but they can be hard to find. One challenge in higher ed is that the expected pathways are so rigidly defined that it's hard to envision different possibilities, let alone pursue them. In that context, how do you even know what you might want to do? Discernment and discovery can be a really difficult part of the process. But it's so important, because it taps into our deep curiosity — which is often part of what brought us to graduate school in the first place. Your desires matter, and your curiosity matters.

Principles for the path

  • Know what you need.
  • Guard your time.
  • Find your people.
  • Seek resources before you need them.
  • Name and own your skills.
The Doctoral Futures research consistently shows that students who persist are those with strong peer networks and at least one faculty advocate. You can build both. And for faculty in the room: your students need you to know what non-academic careers look like, and to not treat career exploration as a sign of failure.

Moving toward joy

We get there by committing to action.

Students: What is one thing you can do right now to support your goals?
Faculty and staff: What can you do to hold space for mycelial work, and to build structures that support it?
Final question: what is one thing you can do — to support your career goals, or to move closer to integrity with your core values? This is not something to put off to a hypothetical less-stressful future. How can you find spaces for nourishment and support even within a context that often feels antithetical to replenishment?
