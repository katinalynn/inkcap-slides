Unexpected Flourishing
Fungi, Failure, and the Future of Higher Ed

What if the organisms best adapted to collapse have something to teach us about how universities — and the people inside them — might actually survive what's coming?
Katina L. Rogers  ·  Inkcap Consulting

Mycelium doesn't grow toward the light — it grows toward what it needs

Fungi navigate by sensing gradients of moisture, nutrients, and chemical signals — not by following a predetermined path. Their growth is responsive, distributed, and almost entirely invisible until something blooms.

  • No central command — the network is the organism
  • Failure in one branch doesn't kill the system; it redirects resources
  • Resilience isn't about bouncing back — it's about what you do with dead matter
The graduate school pipeline was designed like a ladder. Mycelium is a web.
[ Close-up mycelium network photograph — replace with <img> tag ]
This is the conceptual anchor for the whole talk. Don't rush it. The point isn't that grad students are mushrooms — it's that we've been taught to think about career success in the wrong geometry entirely. Let the ladder/web contrast sit.

Decomposition is not the opposite of growth — it is how growth becomes possible

Saprotrophic fungi break down what is dead so that living systems can use it. What looks like failure or ending is, in the fungal world, the precondition for new life.

  • Graduate attrition, derailed tenure cases, careers that "didn't work out" — we treat these as waste
  • Fungi would treat them as substrate — the raw material that feeds what comes next
  • The skills, relationships, and knowledge don't disappear when someone leaves the academy
What if we stopped calling it failure and started asking what it's feeding?

Thriving at the margins is a survival strategy, not a consolation prize

Many of the most ecologically significant fungi — truffles, morels, the fungi that anchor entire forest ecosystems — live in marginal, disturbed, or overlooked zones. They aren't waiting for ideal conditions. They make conditions.

  • Mycorrhizal networks are built on mutual dependency — not competition
  • Edge habitats, where systems meet and blur, produce the richest diversity
  • The alt-ac, post-ac, adjacent-ac career path is an edge habitat — generative, not marginal
The most interesting work in higher education right now is happening at the edges of it.

Part Two

What's Actually Broken
in Higher Education

Setting aside the mythology. The labor market data, stipend realities, and structural inequities that make "just follow your passion" genuinely harmful advice.
Pivot in tone here. Part One was generative and hopeful — part two requires honesty about what we're actually dealing with. Acknowledge that this is uncomfortable, and that's exactly why we need to look at it directly.

The academic job market has not recovered — and is not going to

75%
of all U.S. college courses now taught by contingent faculty — adjuncts, lecturers, and graduate students with no job security
~3%
tenure-track job placement rate in many humanities disciplines — for every opening, dozens of highly qualified candidates compete
$3,900
median per-course adjunct pay in the U.S. — often without benefits, office space, or institutional support of any kind
Sources: American Association of University Professors, The Annual Report on the Economic Status of the Profession, 2023–24; Coalition on the Academic Workforce, A Portrait of Part-Time Faculty Members
These numbers should feel visceral, not abstract. Pause between each card. The per-course pay figure is particularly striking — do the math out loud if the room seems receptive: three courses a semester is still below a living wage in most cities.

Graduate stipends have not kept pace — and the gap is widening

$23K
median annual humanities PhD stipend nationally — often unchanged in real terms over the past decade despite sharply rising costs
1 in 2
PhD students who report experiencing food insecurity, housing instability, or significant financial stress during their program
6–9 yrs
average time to degree in humanities and social sciences — years during which students forego other income and accumulate deferred financial costs
Sources: Council of Graduate Schools, Ph.D. Completion and Attrition; National Science Foundation, Survey of Earned Doctorates, 2023; Hope Center for College, Community, and Justice
This slide often produces silence. Let it. The six-to-nine year figure carries particular weight — you can ask the room: what would you have done with those years, and what did the people who did something else build in the meantime?

The system was designed for a specific kind of person — and most people in it are not that person

The postwar American research university was built on an assumption of the scholar as a solitary, mobile, financially comfortable, typically male, typically white professional. That assumption is embedded in everything from stipend structures to conference cultures to the shape of the academic year.

  • Caregiving responsibilities, geographic constraints, and financial need are treated as individual problems — not structural design failures
  • First-generation scholars face unspoken codes that no one teaches and few will name
  • The attrition we call "not a good fit" often describes a system that was never designed to fit
Telling people to "be more resilient" in a system that produces precarity is not advice. It is blame.
This is the hardest slide to deliver, and the most important. Don't soften it. The orange box is the thesis of this section — let it breathe. If there's energy in the room, invite a response before moving on.
Part Three

What Thriving Actually Looks Like
Beyond Survival Mode

Not a motivational pivot. A practical, structural, and imaginative reorientation toward the conditions that allow people — and institutions — to genuinely flourish.
Signal the shift in register. This section isn't about toxic positivity — it's about taking the structural critique seriously enough to ask what would actually help. Come in with energy, but not false cheerfulness.

Redefining success means refusing someone else's definition of failure

Thriving isn't about landing a tenure-track job or any single destination. It's about building a life in which your intellectual work, your values, and your material needs are in sustainable relationship with one another.

  • Identify what you're actually optimizing for — not what you were told to want
  • Map your actual skills, not just your credentials — the gap is usually larger and more generative than you expect
  • Flourishing looks different in different soil — find yours
The question isn't "did you make it?" It's "what are you building, and does it sustain you?"
[ Mushroom emerging from unexpected substrate — pavement, tree bark, compost — replace with <img> tag ]
This slide works best if it feels personal rather than prescriptive. Talk about what you've learned from your own redefinition. The highlight box is the frame for everything that follows — it's not about achievement, it's about sustainability.

Build lateral networks — not ladders — and make the connections visible

The career ladder assumes a single vertical path upward, with a known destination at the top. The mycelium network assumes multiple directions, shared resources, and the possibility that the most valuable connections run sideways — or underground.

  • Cultivate relationships across sectors, disciplines, and institutional types — not just within your field
  • Share information about salaries, opportunities, and working conditions — opacity protects no one except those already with power
  • Mentorship that only flows downward in a hierarchy is not mentorship — it's reproduction of the existing order
  • The people who help you most may not be the most senior people in the room
A web is stronger than a ladder, and when part of it fails, the rest holds.
This is actionable and should feel that way. You can invite people to think about one lateral connection they could make this week. The transparency point about salaries often generates strong reactions — let people sit with discomfort if it comes up.

Institutions that want to thrive must learn to decompose their own dead matter

Universities that survive the current inflection point won't be the ones that defended every existing structure. They'll be the ones that learned — like fungi — to break down what is no longer living in order to feed what is trying to grow.

  • Meaningful change requires naming what is dead: the postwar enrollment boom, the cheap adjunct labor model, the prestige economy that benefits the few
  • Graduate programs that prepare students only for the tenure track are preparing them for a world that no longer exists
  • Institutions that invest in career diversity, transparency, and cross-sector fluency are not abandoning academic values — they are practicing them
Unexpected flourishing is what happens when you stop defending the rot and start working with it.
