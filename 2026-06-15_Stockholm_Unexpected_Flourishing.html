Part One

A Rotting Log

Universities in their current form are the ruins — the fallen log that provides a substrate for new growth.
This sounds cynical, but I say it as someone deeply invested in higher education — I have a PhD from a public university, worked at CUNY for nearly a decade, and now consult with universities across the country. Even as I work toward reform, I can see that many of the problems go far beyond what any one person or program can address.

The status quo is not working

  • Decades of state disinvestment have left public universities deeply underfunded
  • Most university teaching jobs now go to adjunct faculty — meager wages, no stability, no benefits
  • Tuition has skyrocketed, leaving many students deep in debt
  • Structural inequities — sexism, racism, ableism — persist as universities double down on prestige metrics
  • Now: federal defunding of awarded grants, threats to academic freedom, targeting of international students
And yet — as a society we need the critical thinking, collaboration, and knowledge that universities offer. Maybe more than ever.
[ Photo — something suggesting disruption and resilience: fungi through concrete, or storm-damaged tree still leafing ]
COVID made it very clear that the solo, prestige-oriented model doesn't work anymore. Now we're seeing even more upheaval. The state of upheaval could mean we have a unique opportunity to reinvest while also reimagining how educational structures function. The log has already fallen.
Part Two

Learning with and from Fungi

The current landscape is what it is. But maybe those inherited structures can be digested and neutralized — enabling something new to grow in their midst.
This is where I find hope, and fungi help me get there. They help me look for and find unexpected flourishing — signs of vibrant life where I least expect it.

Blasted landscapes

Matsutake mushroom

  • Grows only in degraded landscapes, when fast-growing pine replaces old-growth forest
  • Cannot be cultivated in a lab or a garden
  • Worth its weight in gold
  • Emerges precisely because of disturbance, not despite it

The critical hope

  • Even from blasted landscapes, something new and precious can emerge
  • Where are the pockets of possibility?
  • How might the digested material bring new life?
  • What interspecies connections bring an unexpected flush of new growth?
Anna Lowenhaupt Tsing, The Mushroom at the End of the World: On the Possibility of Life in Capitalist Ruins (Princeton University Press, 2015)
One of my gateway books into this topic. Tsing follows the lifecycle and global economies of the matsutake mushroom. What I take from this is a kind of critical hope — a hope that even from these blasted landscapes, something new and precious can emerge. This is where I think higher education can learn from fungi.

Mycorenewal

Mycoremediation is the process by which fungi digest and neutralize soil toxins, renewing fertile ground.

  • Hyphae — one cell wide, invisible underground, seeking resources
  • Mycorrhizal networks — channeling nutrients between species, deeply reciprocal
  • Fruiting bodies — ephemeral, surprising, sometimes edible, sometimes deadly
What if the everyday actions of care and resistance in a university are the mycelium — quietly spinning threads of connection even when nobody can see the result?

The metaphor I explore throughout the book. The inherited structures of the university are the rotting log. The mycelium is the hidden, persistent work of connection and care. Sometimes, when conditions are right, a mushroom emerges — nourishment, beauty, possibility.

Part Three

Imagining a
Mycelial University

Rather than investing wholly in the existing apparatus of hierarchy, structure, and prestige, what if we held aside pockets of resources for something else entirely?
What I propose is to let the hierarchical, prestige-oriented model fade to the background in favor of a mycelial university — one that is more fluid and better able to respond to and foster the unexpected bits of growth that crop up outside of where they are supposed to be found.

A Different Value Structure

The prestige university

  • Solo, competitive work — a single flower, with all weeds cleared aside
  • Selectivity, grants, publications as metrics of success
  • Stability and permanence as goals
  • Visibility and recognition as reward

The mycelial university

  • Tiny, hidden growth like hyphae — liminal, small-scale, exploratory
  • Mycorrhizal connections — unexpected partnerships, reciprocal knowledge exchange
  • Projects that bloom and fade, without permanence as expectation
  • Human needs — wellbeing, pleasure, care — built into scholarly success
The spaces where we've seen this so far tend to be in peer-to-peer, often student-led spaces — academic mutual aid, creative interdisciplinary work, publicly engaged research. I don't know that these kinds of organic spaces of growth can be institutionalized without sucking the life out of them, but maybe we can create conditions in which they are more likely to thrive.

The art of noticing

There are nutrients in the compost heap, and protection in the shadows. Shining a spotlight on everything that moves is not a strategy grounded in care.

  • Noticing the commonplace is much harder than noticing novelty
  • The body makes itself known — through pain, deprivation, or pleasure
  • What does pleasure in learning feel like? It is rarely part of academic rhetoric.
  • What is the university equivalent of a slow, quiet mushroom walk?
Building a mycelial university requires relationships, and time, and care.
[ Photo — close-up detail, single mushroom or forest floor ]
Noticing can pierce the opaque cover of things that are invisible because they are ubiquitous. Catching the attention of university senior leadership, or communications and marketing, can suck the life out of a program. Sometimes people are able to thrive precisely because they move unnoticed.

The spores are there; the work is happening — in student-led movements, in peer-to-peer support, in creative interdisciplinary work, in publicly engaged research that embraces community knowledge.

Toward abundance

Taking cues from the abundance mindset of Black and Indigenous feminisms and feminisms of color — can we imagine a scholarly environment not grounded in competitiveness and scarcity?

  • Sometimes it's worth thinking small — countless small interactions where we can have an impact
  • Quiet acts of change, resistance, and support can have immeasurable effect
  • Supporting creative, applied research may also improve the inclusivity of doctoral programs and the scholarly ecosystem
  • The first step is to work differently
My hope is that we can collectively move toward joy — and toward structures that make joy possible.
[ Photo — abundant, vivid mushroom growth ]
Through my students, I learned to value things I might otherwise brush off as small — because they taught me that the quiet acts of change and support can have immeasurable impact. These are modest steps to move toward a different framing, one that emphasizes abundance and thriving.
