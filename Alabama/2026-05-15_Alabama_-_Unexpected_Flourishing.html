Research and Careers in Uncertain Times
Katina L. Rogers  ·  Inkcap Consulting

Why unexpected flourishing?

Thriving doesn't always look the way we expect. What looks like rot is also essential to new growth.

  • Cycles of decay and regeneration
  • Interdependent, multispecies life
  • Mycoremediation — fungi digest and neutralize soil toxins, renewing fertile ground
  • Beauty, even in decay — if you move slowly and look closely
  • For me: critical hope
One way to see this picture is as a rotting log. Another is to see it as a thriving ecosystem. Mushrooms remind me to move slowly, to look closely, to notice. You have to walk slowly, look again. This posture of attention is central to what I'm arguing today.

The rotting log

Universities in their current form are the substrate — the fallen log from which something new might grow.

  • Decades of disinvestment have left public universities deeply underfunded
  • Most teaching jobs now go to contingent faculty — meager wages, no stability, no benefits
  • Tuition has skyrocketed; many students carry debt for years post-degree
  • Structural inequities — sexism, racism, ableism — persist as institutions double down on prestige metrics
The decay is real. It is also a condition for new growth.
This sounds cynical, but it's not despair — it's a diagnosis. And like a fallen log in the forest, the decay is also a condition for new growth. COVID made it very clear that the solo, prestige-oriented model doesn't work. But there's something I want to name more directly today.

A moment of acute precarity

We are facing additional challenges in our current moment:

  • Federal defunding of awarded research grants — the ground is being pulled out mid-project
  • Direct threats to academic freedom, DEI programs, and curricular autonomy
  • Targeting of international students and scholars — devastating for communities and the research ecosystem
  • Chilling effects: scholars self-censoring, programs preemptively dismantling
This is not a normal period of institutional stress. And yet, this is exactly when critical thinking, community, and mycelial connection matter most.
I want to name this directly. Many of you are navigating real fear — about your funding, your programs, your colleagues' visa status, your own research. These are legitimate questions and I don't want to paper over them with optimism. But I do want to offer a framework for thinking about what's possible, even now.

Toward a mycelial university

What if, rather than investing wholly in the apparatus of prestige and hierarchy, we held aside pockets of resources for something else entirely?

  • Hyphae: tiny, hidden, a single cell wide — liminal work, small-scale, exploratory, but also strong and dense
  • Mycorrhizal networks: unexpected partnerships, deeply reciprocal knowledge exchange
  • Fruiting bodies: ephemeral, surprising — projects that bloom without permanence as expectation
  • Mycoremediation: digesting inherited toxins, creating conditions for new growth
Image source: Cartabia, M. et al. 2021. https://doi.org/10.3390/jof7121008
Mycelium is the main component of fungi — threads a single cell wide, growing densely in soil or decaying logs, usually unseen but everywhere under our feet. By "mycelial university" I mean one that fosters tiny, interconnected, often hidden areas of growth. One that makes space for liminal work — work that may or may not bear fruit, but that nourishes the ecosystem.

What the research tells us

1.
Advisor quality is decisive. A supportive advisor is perhaps the most crucial element in student progress and well-being. However, advising practices vary significantly and are not often institutionally rewarded.
2.
Funding is systematically inadequate. Material support is essential to student progress, but the typical stipend falls short of a living wage. Students face food insecurity and impossible trade-offs.
3.
Peer communities are lifelines. Informal peer networks provide crucial support, especially when formal structures fail. Program size reductions are making this increasingly difficult.
4.
Career guidance matters. While grad career advising has improved, for many students the discussion starts toward the end of their studies. It should be integrated into the entire curriculum, starting on day one.
5.
Curricular clarity supports success. Students should know why each step of their program matters. Clear guidelines plus space for creative work leads to stronger progress and more meaningful research.

My anchor points

Moving from individual action to structural change.

  • Talking about career diversity is not enough — we need structural change in how programs define and measure success
  • Structures reveal and reinforce values — an economy of prestige often undermines values of equity
  • Public engagement is good for students, the academy, and society
  • Joy and sustainability are possible — but not without acknowledging systems of power and working toward equity
This is an equity question.
Who decides what is considered valuable? Whose voices are heard?

I've been working on graduate education reform for almost fifteen years, and the throughline looks so much clearer in retrospect than it did when I was moving through it. I was always just making the best decision I could within the context and constraints of the moment.

Redefining success

The prestige model

  • Replication of what has come before
  • Primarily oriented toward research outcomes; teaching and mentorship are secondary
  • "Hidden curriculum" advantages those who already know the system
  • Hostile environment and high attrition for historically marginalized students and faculty

A broader value structure

  • Explicit mentorship training and accountability structures
  • Holistic admissions motivated by cohort-building and the post-PhD landscape, not just academic pedigree
  • Emphasis on creativity, publicly engaged research, collaborative and relational work
  • Attention to human needs — wellbeing, pleasure, care — as part of scholarly success
Cultural change cannot happen without structural change.
Higher ed is oriented around prestige, and that makes it very hard to make space for other value systems. This is where the hidden curriculum comes into play — some people come in knowing more about the system than others. There are ways to foster greater equity and more creative scholarship just by breaking down some of these tacit expectations.

Navigating
Where We Are

Some of this is harm reduction. The system is broken. Grad students have an important voice in reform — but you're also in a precarious position. It's important to be a bit selfish.
I want to be practical here. Not because the structural questions don't matter — they do — but because you need tools to navigate where you are right now, not just a vision for where we hope to get.

Where do I even start?

When you're trying to move in new directions, it can be hard to know where to put your energy. Here are some key focal points:

  • Assess what's working for you and what's not
  • Build relationships and learn the landscape
  • Seek out resources before you need them
  • Make a plan
  • Put yourself out there!
  • Take time to reflect
  • Repeat.

What does thriving look like for you?

Forget what formal academic success is supposed to look like. Take one minute to jot down:
  • A book, film, or song you love
  • A person whose work you admire — work defined broadly
  • One thing you'd spend more time on if you could
Give them a genuine minute. Then ask: what patterns do you see? Which questions were hard, and why? Do these responses align with what you see in higher ed? The way you respond gives a glimpse of what thriving looks like for you — and taking that seriously is part of how we shift collectively from survival mode toward the possibility of flourishing.

Principles for the path

  • Know what you need. Return to that question of what thriving looks like. Don't lose sight of it, even when the system discourages you from asking.
  • Guard your time. You don't have to say yes to everything. Discernment matters — not every opportunity is worth taking.
  • Find your people. Peers, mentors, collaborators — these relationships sustain us professionally and personally. They are not a luxury.
  • Use resources before you need them. Career offices, alt-ac networks, tools like Imagine PhD — engage early, not in crisis mode.
  • Name your skills. Teaching, writing, research design, community engagement — practice articulating these in multiple registers.

The Doctoral Futures research consistently shows that students who persist are those with strong peer networks and at least one faculty advocate. You can build both. And for faculty in the room: your students need you to know what non-academic careers look like, and to not treat career exploration as a sign of failure.

Sometimes we need to think small. Yes, we need broad-scale change. But rather than become frozen by how daunting that seems, sometimes it's worth zooming in to think about the countless small interactions where we CAN have an impact. Through my students, I learned to value things I might otherwise brush off as small — because quiet acts of change, resistance, and support can have immeasurable impact. adrienne maree brown describes this in Emergent Strategy as working in a fractal way: the patterns we're working toward at the broad scale should also be present in the smallest ones.

Moving toward joy

Taking cues from the abundance mindset of Black and Indigenous feminisms and feminisms of color — can we imagine a scholarly environment that is not grounded in competitiveness and scarcity?

    My hope is that we can collectively move toward joy — and toward structures that make joy possible.
