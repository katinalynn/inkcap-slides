Welcome and frame the session. We've just spent time on the big picture — now let's get into the practical questions faculty actually face. This is a conversation, not a lecture.

Three questions I hear most from faculty

These are the questions that have come up repeatedly as I've worked with faculty across institutions:

  • How do I offer guidance on pathways I've never experienced?
  • How can I support vulnerable students in the current climate?
  • How can our department simultaneously hold rigorous standards and be open to new ways of working?
What else are you carrying in with you today?
Reveal each question one click at a time. Pause after each one — invite a nod, a reaction. These are meant to feel like recognition. End with the highlight box to signal this is a back-and-forth, not a presentation.

Advising students into the unknown

You don't need to have walked the path to help someone find their way. Effective advising starts with three things:

  • Curiosity
  • Connection
  • Guidance
Mentor mapping — identifying who plays what role in a student's network — is a simple, powerful place to start.

Spend a few minutes here before opening to discussion. The mentor mapping point is worth dwelling on — it's a concrete tool faculty can use in advising conversations. Consider pausing to ask: "What does your own mentor map look like? Who's in the room for your students?"

Supporting students in challenging circumstances

International students, students with precarious status, and others navigating structural barriers need the same core things — with extra attentiveness.

  • Ask what's most top of mind; don't assume you know what they're facing
  • Know where to find institutional resources before a crisis makes them urgent
  • Connect with local organizers who understand the specific terrain
Your role isn't to solve every problem — it's to make sure students don't have to face them entirely alone.
[ Screenshot — institutional resource page, grad school support guide, or relevant org ]
This section may bring up anxiety in the room — that's appropriate. Acknowledge it. The point isn't to have all the answers, but to have done enough advance work to be a reliable resource. Ask: "Do you know what resources your institution has? Has that changed recently?"

Rigor and openness are not opposites

The false opposition

  • Difficulty is not the same as quality
  • Gatekeeping rarely develops scholars — it mostly filters them
  • Narrowing too soon is a risk, not a virtue

Practical strategies

  • Start with clarity: what are the requirements, and why?
  • Build lower-stakes moments for exploration into coursework
  • Draw on existing pedagogical resources — you don't have to invent this
Some of the most creative work is also the most rigorous. The goal is to cultivate both.
This often generates the most animated discussion. The false opposition framing may push back on assumptions some faculty hold strongly — approach with curiosity, not confrontation. Ask: "Where do you feel tension between rigor and openness in your own teaching or mentorship?"

Your own wellbeing matters too

Faculty face real structural pressures — precarity, overwork, competing demands on time and energy.

  • Honest acknowledgment of constraints is not defeatism — it's clarity
  • Sustainable practices in your own work make genuine mentorship possible
  • Setting limits models something essential for students who struggle to do the same
The way you navigate these challenges teaches students at least as much as your curriculum does.
This slide often lands quietly. Give it space. Faculty are not used to being asked how they're doing. You can ask directly: "What does sustainability look like in your own work right now? What would you want to model for your students — and what's getting in the way?"

Let's talk

In small groups or as a full room — take 15–20 minutes with one or more of these:
  • When has a student surprised you with where they wanted to go? How did you respond?
  • Where do you feel least equipped in advising right now — and what would actually help?
  • What are you already doing well that you could do more intentionally?
Give the room genuine time here — don't rush to debrief. If energy is high in small groups, let it run a bit long. When you pull back together, ask one person from each table to share a thread rather than a summary. The goal is to surface questions, not close them down.
